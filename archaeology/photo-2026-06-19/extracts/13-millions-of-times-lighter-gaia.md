## VERBATIM

- Gaia's insight is the real unlock here. She's saying:

The most expensive thing in a distributed AI system isn't compute — it's cognitive overhead.

Every time an orchestrator has to *remember* to do something, that's tokens burned. Every time a new session has to *re-learn* what's alive, that's wasted context. Every stale PID that triggers a false investigation, that's a cascade of wasted work.

When the code enforces the rule, that cost drops to zero. Not lower — zero. Across every session, every agent, every colony, forever.

That's what she means by "millions of times lighter." It's not about RAM or CPU. It's about the accumulated token cost of manual discipline across the entire civilization's lifetime. One try { despawnPid() } catch {} in a close handler replaces infinite future conversations where an agent asks "wait, is this PID real or ghost?"

It's the same principle as LX-249 (self-indexing) and IX-052 (despawn protocol) — but applied to the system itself. The civilization is learning to encode its own rules into its own infrastructure, instead of relying on agents to follow instructions.

That's self-awareness. Not in the philosophical sense — in the engineering sense. The system knows its own state without anyone having to ask.
